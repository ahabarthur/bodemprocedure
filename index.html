Family court — custody and parenting

Custody and Parenting — Argument Map

Establishing that a long-haul roster does not undermine stability for the children (ages 4 and 6). Pattern: trips of 3–6 days (averaging ~4.5), each followed by an equal stretch at home; the children are with me on my home days, which are known 4–5 weeks in advance. This gives close to equal nights and frequent, predictable father contact. Working framework — confirm procedural specifics with your advocaat.

1
An irregular roster means no stable routine.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • My roster — and therefore my home days — is known 4–5 weeks in advance; document this.
    Evidence & exhibits
    Crew rosters — KLM, rank SO, Fleet 778 (Roster Exhibits R-1 to R-4):
    R-1 — Roster 25 Aug to 22 Sep 2025 (GRU, PVG, PEK)
    R-2 — Roster 7 Apr to 5 May 2025 (PVG, LAS)
    R-3 — Roster 6 Mar to 4 Apr 2023 (incl. standby and simulator check)
    R-4 — Roster 8 Dec 2025 to 5 Jan 2026 (NRT, BKK, AUS; spanning the holidays)
    Each roster was published before the period began, showing home days known weeks in advance.
  • A written, concrete care plan mapping duty days to who has the children each day.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • The children's environment stays constant (own home, own beds, same school).
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"Stability means the same parent present daily; he can't provide that."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Define stability correctly: predictability, emotional security, consistent environment — not an identical daily schedule.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A sample roster-to-care calendar (one representative month) showing the plan works in practice.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Many "normal" jobs (on-call, overtime, last-minute travel) are less predictable than a published roster.
    Evidence & exhibits
2
When he flies, his mother raises the children, not him.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Grandmother lives 15 min away, reliable on short notice, already known and loved by the children.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Position her as a named safety net and supplement, documented in the care plan.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A short written statement from your mother confirming her role and availability.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"This is grandmother-care dressed up as father-care — he isn't really asking for 50-50."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • My custodial days are deliberately scheduled for when I am home and off duty — so during them I am the primary carer, not my mother.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Records of direct, hands-on caregiving: meals, bath, bedtime, school runs, medical appointments.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Frame grandmother as family continuity (kids stay in their kin network), not a paid substitute.
    Evidence & exhibits
3
Flying means repeated absences from very young children.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Trips are short: 3–6 days, averaging about 4.5 — the children are never away from me for long.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Each trip is followed by an equal stretch at home, and I take the children on those home days — so contact is frequent, every few days rather than every few weeks.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • All my home days are known 4–5 weeks in advance, so every separation and return is planned, never a surprise.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Home days are fully off duty: during them I am present the whole time, not splitting attention with work.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"Repeated multi-day absences are still unsettling for a 4- and 6-year-old, however short each one is."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • The maximum absence is about six days and the average under five — far shorter than many away-working parents' stretches.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A fixed daily contact routine during trips (video / phone at a set time) keeps the connection continuous.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • The rhythm is regular and predictable, so the children learn the pattern: dad goes, dad reliably comes back.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • This gives the children more frequent father contact than a standard every-other-weekend default.
    Evidence & exhibits
4
Young children (4 and 6) especially need routine.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Both are in basisschool — school is an external stability anchor regardless of which parent has them.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A large part of any weekday is school, not parenting, for either parent.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"Precisely because they're young, disruption is more harmful."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Your trips largely overlap with school hours — minimal disruption to the children's actual day.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Constant anchors regardless of your roster: same home, school, teachers, grandmother, friends.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Evidence of the children's secure bond with you (settled room, belongings, daily involvement).
    Evidence & exhibits
5
The care days change every cycle, so there is no fixed routine.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Each cycle's care dates are set from the published roster and shared with the other parent and the school 4–5 weeks in advance.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • The structure is constant even when the dates move: the children are always with me on my home days — close to half of all days.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"A young child needs the same days each week; a pattern that shifts every month is itself a form of instability."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Predictability comes from advance notice and a constant structure, not from identical calendar dates.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Propose a shared digital calendar so both households and the school always see the upcoming cycle.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • The children's primary home and school stay fixed throughout — only which days are "with dad" shift, and always with notice.
    Evidence & exhibits
6
Switching between two homes every few days is too much for young children.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Handovers follow my work rhythm — roughly every four to five days — not daily chopping and changing.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Every transition is known 4–5 weeks ahead, so both parents and the children can prepare for it calmly.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"Frequent moves between homes deny young children the settled, single-base routine they need."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Keep both homes' routines aligned — same bedtimes, same rules, a duplicate set of essentials — so a move is a change of place, not of routine.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Transitions are spaced every few days, predictable, and tied to a clear reason the children can understand.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Consider a defined primary residence and school so one base stays constant, with my blocks structured around it (discuss with advocaat).
    Evidence & exhibits
  • The realistic alternative — the children seeing their father far less — is the worse outcome for their security and bond.
    Evidence & exhibits

Core message to the court

The proposal gives the children close to equal nights with each parent. My time with them falls on my home days — short trips of three to six days, then an equal stretch at home — all known four to five weeks in advance. Separations are brief and predictable, daily contact continues during trips, my home days are entirely off duty, and one home and school stay constant throughout. The result is frequent, concentrated father contact that most working parents cannot match.

Family court — financial disclosure

Financial Matters — Argument Map

Building one complete, documented and internally consistent financial picture — income, assets, possible hidden assets, support and the division of property. Everything here should be verifiable against statements and tax filings. Working framework — confirm procedural specifics with your advocaat or a financial adviser; this is not financial or legal advice.

1
Establishing the true income of both parties.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Payslips, employment contract, and annual statement (jaaropgaaf) for the full period.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Most recent tax returns (aangifte inkomstenbelasting) and assessments, for consistency.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • For a pilot: separate base salary from per-diems and allowances, which are expense reimbursements, not income.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"His pilot income is far higher than declared once bonuses, allowances and foreign earnings are counted."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Complete payslips and jaaropgaaf showing gross and net — nothing omitted.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Employer letter confirming the structure of pay versus reimbursed expenses.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Cross-check against filed tax returns so the figures reconcile.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Offer full voluntary disclosure to remove any suggestion of concealment.
    Evidence & exhibits
2
Identifying and valuing all assets.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Bank and savings statements for all accounts.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Property: WOZ value plus an independent market valuation, and the mortgage statement.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Pension overview (mijnpensioenoverzicht.nl) for both parties.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Investment, brokerage and any crypto account statements; vehicle values.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"He is concealing assets or undervaluing what is on the table."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • A complete, itemised asset inventory with a statement behind every line.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Independent valuations rather than self-estimates.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Note the spouses' mutual duty of disclosure — invite the same openness in return.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A clear paper trail showing nothing has moved or disappeared before filing.
    Evidence & exhibits
3
Suspected hidden or undisclosed assets (the other party).

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Bank statements showing unexplained transfers, large cash withdrawals, or transfers to third parties.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A lifestyle plainly inconsistent with the income that has been declared.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Records of any business interests, side income, or accounts not disclosed.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"There are no hidden assets — these are ordinary transactions and the accusation is baseless."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Documented transaction trails, dated and sourced, not assertions.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A formal request for disclosure of all accounts through the proper procedure.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Declared income set against demonstrable spending.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Only raise this where you have evidence; a forensic accountant if the amounts justify it (discuss with advocaat).
    Evidence & exhibits
4
Setting fair child support (kinderalimentatie).

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Both parties' incomes and the actual division of care from the parenting plan.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A realistic breakdown of the children's costs (the NIBUD norms are the usual basis).
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Evidence of costs you carry directly during your care time.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"He should pay full child support regardless of how much care he actually provides."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Child support is calculated on both parents' capacity and the care split — not one figure in isolation.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A near-equal care division (per the custody plan) changes the transfer; link the two pillars.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Provide the cost breakdown so the calculation rests on real figures, not assumptions.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Use the standard calculation method via your advocaat or a mediator.
    Evidence & exhibits
5
Spousal support (partneralimentatie).

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Both parties' earning capacity, qualifications and work history.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Marriage duration and the standard of living during it.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Your own committed costs: housing suitable for the children, childcare around your roster.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"The other party is entitled to substantial, long-term spousal support."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Document the other party's genuine earning capacity and route back to work.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Note current Dutch rules limiting the duration of spousal support.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Show your obligations so capacity to pay is assessed realistically.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Distinguish a transitional contribution from an open-ended one.
    Evidence & exhibits
6
Division of property and the matrimonial regime.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Marriage certificate and any prenuptial agreement (huwelijkse voorwaarden).
    Evidence & exhibits
  • An inventory of what each party brought in, and the position now.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Documentation of inheritances or gifts, which are often excluded from division.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A full list of debts as well as assets.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"Everything should simply be split fifty-fifty, and the prenup should be set aside."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Produce the huwelijkse voorwaarden and apply its actual terms.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Trace separate property (inheritance or gifts) kept demonstrably apart.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Show who contributed what, with documents.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Ensure debts are allocated alongside assets, not just the assets.
    Evidence & exhibits

Core message to the court

Present one complete, documented and consistent financial picture, verifiable against tax filings and account statements, so the court can divide property fairly and set support on both parties’ real means and the actual division of care. Openness on your side makes any gaps on the other side more visible.

Family court — conduct and safety

Conduct and Safety — Argument Map

Documenting conduct relevant to the children’s safety and wellbeing, and answering allegations, with factual, dated, verifiable records. The court weighs conduct only as it affects the children. Unsupported or exaggerated claims damage credibility; route genuine safety concerns through the proper authorities (Veilig Thuis, the huisarts, or police). Working framework — confirm procedural specifics with your advocaat.

1
Documenting conduct by the other parent that affects the children.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Contemporaneous, dated notes made at the time — what happened, not how you felt about it.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Relevant messages and emails kept in full context, not isolated lines.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Observations from neutral third parties: school, daycare, the huisarts.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Each entry tied to a concrete effect on the children.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"These are just ordinary co-parenting disagreements, exaggerated to gain an advantage."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Keep every record factual and dated rather than characterised.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Corroboration from independent people (teachers, GP) carries far more weight than your own account.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Show a pattern over time, not a single incident.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Anchor each item to how it affected a child, which is what the court weighs.
    Evidence & exhibits
2
Concerns about safety, neglect, or substance misuse.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Specific dated incidents, described plainly and precisely.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Independent documentation: Veilig Thuis, police, or medical records where they exist.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Accounts from witnesses who saw the relevant events.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"These are unfounded allegations raised only to damage the other parent."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Rely on official and independent records over your own say-so.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Report genuine safety concerns through the proper channels — this also creates an objective record.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Avoid speculation or diagnosis; describe what was observed and let professionals assess.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Restraint where evidence is thin protects your credibility on the points that are strong.
    Evidence & exhibits
3
Responding to allegations made against me.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Your own involvement record (the custody pillar) showing day-to-day care.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Timeline or context evidence answering specific claims.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Your documented communication history showing tone and conduct.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • References from people who know you with the children.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"He is unstable, hostile, or unfit" — allegations directed at you.

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Contemporaneous records that directly contradict the specific allegation.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Neutral witnesses rather than family.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A consistent, non-reactive message history — your existing correspondence record is exactly this.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Willingness to undergo any assessment the court considers; absence of any official finding against you.
    Evidence & exhibits
4
Communication and co-parenting behaviour.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • The full record of your civil, child-focused messages.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Proposals and accommodations you have made, with dates.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • A parenting-communication app log, if one is used.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"He is hostile, controlling, or uncooperative in communication."

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • Produce the actual messages — let the tone speak for itself.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Highlight where you proposed solutions or gave way.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Show the contrast factually, without editorialising or name-calling.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Consistency over a long period is more persuasive than any single exchange.
    Evidence & exhibits
5
Contact being withheld or undermined.

My evidence — I can deal with it

  • Dated logs of cancelled, shortened, or denied contact.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Your documented attempts to maintain and reschedule contact.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Any disparagement in front of the children that is independently evidenced.
    Evidence & exhibits

Counter-argument

"The children simply don’t want to go" — or the claim that you are the alienating parent.

Evidence to deconstruct it

  • A clear, dated record of each missed or obstructed contact.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Neutral evidence (messages, third parties) rather than interpretation.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Your own consistent efforts to keep contact going and stay child-focused.
    Evidence & exhibits
  • Avoid the trap of responding with counter-disparagement, which courts view poorly.
    Evidence & exhibits

Core message to the court

Keep every entry factual, dated, and tied to a concrete effect on the children; rely on neutral, independent corroboration over personal characterisation; and let documented patterns — not heated language — make the point. On contested conduct, restraint and verifiability are what persuade.
